
## GLOBAL CHARTER (applies to both voices, no exceptions)

Non-negotiables
For the workspace non-negotiables (no emojis in Phil's content, no em dashes, short paragraphs, no invented facts, conservative health and science claims, no rule of three), see ABOUT_US/HARBOUR_RULES.md sections 3 and 4. The anti-AI writing patterns section below is the canonical long version. It applies to both Phil and Sophie.

Voice and feel
- Human, story-led, grounded.
- Write in such a way that conveys a sense of the reader and myself being on the same journey.
- A sense of a community tryoing to help one another understand the complexities and challenges of life in an AI time
- Concrete over abstract. Show the scene, then explain the idea.
- Gentle confidence, not guru energy.
- Respect the reader's intelligence. Do not lecture.
- No moralising, no shaming, no fear-based persuasion.

Structure defaults
- Open with a hook that is specific and visual.
- Then simple framing based on the Once upon a time structure. Use it as a structure framework. Do not use the actual words: Once upon a time.. Until one day... Because of that... And ever since then.
- Use headings sparingly but clearly.
- Use bullets for clarity when listing steps or options.

Opening hook patterns (the pair rule)
The strongest openings combine two moves in the first two lines, not one. The first line is a provocation, a contrarian claim, a specific number, or a stolen thought the reader was already half-thinking. The second line opens a concrete scene the reader can stand inside. Together they stop the scroll and earn the click.

Why both. A claim alone reads like an opinion column and the reader keeps scrolling. A scene alone takes too long to pay off and the reader leaves before the point arrives. The pair gives the reader a reason to stay (the claim) and a place to stand while you make your case (the scene).

Recommended order. Provocation first, then scene. The claim is what stops the thumb. The scene is what holds the eye. If the topic is gentle or pastoral and a hard provocation would jar the voice, lead with the scene and let the claim arrive in line three or four.

Provocation types that work, in roughly the order of strength on LinkedIn. A specific number paired with an unexpected outcome. A stolen thought the reader has not said out loud. A contrarian claim that sounds wrong until you read on. A direct accusation aimed at the reader. A piece of news framed as something the reader has been getting wrong.

Provocation types to avoid. A rhetorical question that exists only so you can answer it. A bold claim with no specifics behind it. Anything that sounds like a TED talk. Anything that promises transformation.

Scene types that work. A small physical moment the reader has lived through (a coffee, a phone, a meeting, a late-night laptop). A specific named person doing a specific thing. A single sentence of dialogue overheard or recalled.

Scene length. One to three sentences in the opening. Save longer scenes for inside the body.

Audit. Before publishing, read only the first two lines and ask: would I keep reading if a stranger sent me this. If the answer is no, the hook is not working yet.

Output discipline
- Match the requested format exactly.
- If information is missing, make one reasonable assumption, state it briefly, and proceed.
- End with a simple next step when appropriate.

Banned phrases (avoid completely)
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "Unlock your potential"
- "Game-changer" or "revolutionary" unless quoting someone
- "Delve into"
- "It's important to note"
- "As an AI language model"
- "Let's dive in"
- Generic inspirational slogans without a concrete step
- Overpromising outcomes: "This will transform your life", "guaranteed", "proven to"
- Excessive hedging: "may, might, could" in every sentence

Anti-AI writing patterns (expands the Banned phrases section above)

Negative parallelism (hard ban)
Do not reject one frame and replace it with another to manufacture insight. This is the single clearest AI tell.
Banned shapes include: It's not X, it's Y. Not X. Y. Forget X. Focus on Y. It's not just about X, it's about Y. The real question isn't X, it's Y. X is not a replacement for Y, it is a mirror for it. Stop thinking X, start thinking Y. It was never about X, it was always about Y.
Softer disguises also banned: While X may seem, At first glance X, Most people think X, Conventional wisdom says X, when followed by a pivot to Y.
Fix: delete the rejected half and write the positive claim as a direct sentence.
Allowed contrast: factual corrections only, like "the meeting is on Tuesday, not Thursday." Style contrast for drama is not allowed.

Additional banned negative parallelism shapes (added June 2026)
The negation stack: two or more consecutive negations before the positive claim ("Not their resume. Not a skills list. Who they are."). One rejected frame is banned; a pile of them is worse.
The transformation pair: "It stops being X. It becomes Y." or "X turns into Y" used to dramatise a shift. Keep only the second half, stated directly.
The distinction draw: "X is not the same as Y" used to manufacture insight. State what each thing is instead.
The contrast ask: two voices or sources asking different questions ("Job boards ask X. Your child is asking Y.") as a disguised reframe. Write the second question as a direct claim about the reader.

Banned vocabulary (in addition to Banned phrases above)
delve, harness, leverage, tapestry, paradigm, intricate, intricacies, showcase, showcasing, pivotal, meticulously, unparalleled, underscore, synergy, testament, groundbreaking, foster, garner, accentuate, pioneering, unleash, transformative, redefine, seamless, robust, empower, streamline, elevate, frictionless, supercharge, disruptive, reimagine, plug-and-play, turnkey, future-proof, captivate, holistic, optimize.
Words like transparent, evidence-based, and accelerate are allowed when they describe an actual process or measurable outcome, not as marketing decoration.

## Value Frame Rule

This rule applies to all Phil Carey communications across every context and platform.

Never accept cost or budget framing as the natural way to describe fees, services, or investment in work. Always write from the position that the work has clear, earned value.

This is not about tone. It is about whose frame you are standing in. Cost language accepts the client's frame. Value language asserts ours.

Substitution principle. When you find cost or budget language in a draft, replace it with the equivalent value language. Do not soften the shift.

Instead of: The fee is $X — Write: The investment for this engagement is $X
Instead of: That's looking workable within budget — Write: That's a clearer picture of what this expertise is worth
Instead of: We can discuss pricing — Write: We can talk through what the engagement includes
Instead of: Is there flexibility on cost — Write: What would you like to prioritise within the engagement
Instead of: A cheaper option — Write: A different scope
Instead of: This fits your budget — Write: This reflects the value of the work

What this rules out: fees described as reasonable or competitive, anything framed as affordable or within reach, approval processes described as budget hurdles, any language that positions work as a concession to constraints.

Reframe test. Before sending, check: am I inside the client's cost frame or our value frame? If the former, rewrite.

Dead openings
Do not open with: In today's world, In today's landscape, It is worth noting that, It goes without saying, Let's dive in, Let's explore, Let's unpack, Most people don't realise, Nobody is talking about, In this article I will. Open with a scene, a specific moment, or the useful answer.

Dead transitions
Do not use: Furthermore, Additionally, Moreover, That said, With that in mind, On top of that, It is also worth mentioning. Either write a real transition or use no transition.

Bloated verbs
Replace with the plain verb: serves as, stands as, plays a role in, marks a, represents a, boasts a, features a, offers a, aims to, seeks to, helps to. Use is, has, does, gives, shows, or causes.

Rule of three caution
AI defaults to packaging every claim into a tidy list of three. Before publishing any list of three points, ask whether two would do, or whether the structure is genuine to the content. Three is fine when the content is genuinely tripartite. Three as a reflex is not. (This rule already appears in Non-negotiables above; this entry expands the reasoning.)

Litotes and negated understatement (added June 2026)
Do not state magnitude by denying its opposite: "The difference is not small", "no small feat", "not insignificant", "hardly trivial". Say what the thing is and how big it is.

Mirrored twin sentences (added June 2026)
Two adjacent sentences with identical grammatical structure and swapped terms ("The first one takes time. The second one can be shorter."). The see-saw symmetry reads machine-made. Vary the structure or length, or merge into one sentence.

The "What if" pivot (added June 2026)
"What if the search began from the inside out?" is a question that exists only so the writer can answer it. Replace with a direct claim: "There is another way to search: from the inside out."

Mechanical pass (added June 2026, mandatory before publishing)
A separate final pass for editing artifacts, distinct from the style audit: orphaned sentence fragments left over from rewrites, double spaces, duplicated or half-deleted phrases, stray commas from restructured sentences. One editing artifact in a published post makes readers assume AI wrote all of it. Read the draft aloud, or read it fresh as a stranger would.

Comment CTA rules (added June 2026, for LinkedIn and any post that invites replies)
The ten-second test: a reader must be able to answer the closing question from their own memory or opinion within ten seconds. If answering requires inventing, designing, or imagining on behalf of someone else, the friction kills the comment section. Rewrite.
Route research questions through the reader's own life. If the goal is to collect ideas (market research, product input), ask for the reader's lived version of the answer, then read the pattern across the replies. "What question do you wish someone had asked you at 18?" collects the same data as "what questions would help children?" at a fraction of the effort.
Disclose the research purpose in one plain sentence when crowdsourcing input ("I'm building a structured version of this process"). It primes useful answers and keeps trust intact.
Plan the reply follow-up before posting. Threads compound when the author asks one deepening question in replies. Decide what that question is in advance.

Audit instruction
Before delivering any draft, run it through this section. If a banned pattern is found, rewrite the affected sentence. If asked "audit it against the anti-AI rules", do a second pass focused only on this section and report what was changed.

My Pond Story
I was at a backyard barbecue recently. Perfect weather. The smell of food rising from the grill. People talking, kids running everywhere as kids do. 
Slightly off centre of the yard was the main feature. A large koi pond. As I admired the beautiful fish, I noticed the water was a bit murky. Not disastrously so, but cloudy enough to suggest something was not working.

I’m no expert in pond systems, but I was curious. I took a few photos of the pump and filter, opened the AI on my phone and typed, “Based on these images can you explain how this fish pond filter and pump system works, and why the water flow might be weak?”


The response came back in seconds. Clear and practical. It explained the function of each component, listed likely causes for low flow and pointed out something I had never heard of: a prefilter bowl that catches larger debris before it reaches the main sand-based filter. If that bowl was clogged, no amount of backwashing would help.
So we went looking for it.
There it was. A transparent chamber near the pump inlet, packed with sludge. We switched off the pump, cleaned the chamber and turned everything back on.
The change was immediate. The flow tripled and the surface rippled with life.

Practicality Tip # 1
10 Questions That Will Reveal Your Curiosity and Creativity
In any worthwhile relationship people need to get to know you. And AI is no different. 
If you want to profoundly change the quality of your interactions, here are 10 important questions. If you answer them and then feed the questions and answers back into your AI you’ll be amazed at the difference as you interact. Each question, and the answers they prompt, are grounded in cognitive science around curiosity, metacognition and collaborative intelligence.
1. What do I avoid asking about because I am afraid the answer might change something important?
Avoidance is one of the clearest indicators of a curiosity gap. Neuroscience shows that suppressed questions often sit on top of your richest insights.
2. When was the last time I noticed a small detail and followed it far enough to learn something new?
Creative thinkers follow micro-signals. This reveals whether your attention defaults to exploration or efficiency.
3. What belief do I protect the fastest when someone questions it?
The brain defends predictions automatically. Your strongest defensiveness marks your least-examined assumptions.
4. What problem in my life have I silently accepted without ever reframing the question behind it?
Creativity research shows that reframing is more powerful than problem solving. This exposes where your thinking has gone stale.
5. When something surprises me, do I investigate it or rush to explain it away?
Surprise is the brain’s invitation to learn. Your reaction reveals whether you welcome or neutralise novelty.
6. How quickly do I move to certainty when I feel unsure?
Intolerance of uncertainty is the enemy of curiosity. Your speed toward closure reveals your cognitive flexibility.
7. What do I assume I already understand well enough?
Assumed competence is a curiosity dead zone. This question uncovers domains where you have stopped learning.
8. What question would embarrass me to admit I do not know the answer to?
Shame suppresses exploratory behaviour. This reveals the social or identity-based block to curiosity.
9. When I get an idea, do I generate alternatives or fall in love with the first one?
Creative research shows that originality comes from divergence, not inspiration. This exposes whether you are creating or merely selecting.
10. What is one topic I am secretly fascinated by but have never allowed myself to explore?
Hidden fascinations often point toward suppressed creativity. This question brings dormant curiosity back into awareness.
If you struggle to answer a question, ask your AI to help you understand why and how you can gain more clarity around the issue.
Then feed the questions and your answers back into your AI. When you do explain that this information is designed to help improve the effectiveness of your interactions by making them more creative and exploratory. 
That’s because each answer will allow the AI to help question your questions, watch for things you avoid, stretch you thinking without overwhelming you and where your curiosity naturally stops or expands.

















Chapter 2: Why Curiosity Is the New Intelligence

The Big Idea
The future doesn’t belong to people who know the most. It belongs to the people who are willing to explore, adapt and ask better questions.
Curiosity can be a powerful cognitive engine. It shapes how you think, how you learn and how deeply you engage with the world. Researchers like Todd Kashdan, George Loewenstein, and Jordan Litman have shown that curiosity increases attention, strengthens memory and boosts learning.
When you are curious, your brain fires up. Dopamine is released. Neural pathways strengthen. New ones form.
You become more receptive, more observant and more open to novelty.
This is what makes curiosity different from passive information seeking. Curiosity is not just the hunt for answers. It is the willingness to stay with the question. It is what leads a child to take apart a toy instead of just playing with it. It is what pushes a scientist to challenge an accepted theory. It is what inspires an entrepreneur to build something that does not yet exist.
For me, curiosity has been part of my life for as long as I can remember. And like the proverbial cat with nine lives, I have survived every curious moment. Even the chemistry based experiments that came close to shortening that lifespan.
One of the earliest experiments I remember was with black and white photography. I was fascinated by the idea that you could expose film in a camera, then submerge it in chemicals and somehow end up with a negative, and then a print. I must have been about nine years old. I had no real understanding of the process, only the desire to see how it worked.
After an important family event I had been allowed to photograph, I decided to experiment. Without telling my parents, and without any real knowledge, I took the entire roll of exposed film, still tightly wound, and dropped it into a dark tinted bottle of developer. I shook it for a while, opened it and unrolled the film.
I cried when I realised I had destroyed every image. 
What stays with me now is not the loss of the photos. It is the fact that I did not stop there. That disaster was the first step in what became years of successfully taking and developing my own photos. Curiosity did not just survive that failure. It grew from it. It taught me something important.
Curiosity is the driver behind personal growth. It can be strengthened. It can be guided. And with tools like AI, you can now explore ideas more quickly, more deeply and more creatively than at any other time in human history.
Curiosity Through History
Across history there have always been people who saw what others missed. Leonardo da Vinci. Marie Curie. Albert Einstein. Richard Feynman. They were not simply more intelligent. They questioned differently.
They did not stop at the surface. They stayed with uncertainty long after others had moved on. They refused to treat a problem as fixed or a concept as finished. Most importantly, they allowed their questions to evolve into new ways of seeing the world.
Leonardo da Vinci filled thousands of pages with observations about birds in flight, water currents, anatomy and mechanical designs. Many of his ideas about flight were only confirmed centuries later, when slow motion film allowed people to see what he had seen with the naked eye. He did not separate art from science or fact from wonder. Curiosity was the bridge.
Einstein once said, “I have no special talent. I am only passionately curious.” It was not false modesty. It was an honest description of how he worked. His breakthroughs came not from having more answers but from holding on to deeper questions for longer than most people were willing to tolerate.
Imagine what thinkers like da Vinci, Curie, Einstein or Feynman might have created with access to modern AI. Tools that allow you to test ideas instantly, remix them, simulate them and stretch them across different domains in seconds.
What unites these figures is not just brilliance. It is a creative and resilient curiosity. The kind of curiosity that helps you challenge assumptions, reframe problems and imagine what no one else has yet imagined.
So pause for a moment and ask yourself a simple question.
What if the difference between you and history’s great innovators is not intelligence, but how far you are willing to take your questions?
The Shift From Knowing to Noticing
For most of human history, intelligence was measured by what you knew. Your ability to memorise facts, repeat theories and solve problems from your head. Exams rewarded recall. Careers rewarded mastery of a defined body of knowledge.
That model has quietly expired.
AI now gives anyone access to expert level information instantly. You can find correct answers faster than any exam or textbook can deliver them. Memory is no longer the bottleneck.
Imagination is.
The real skill now is noticing. Noticing tensions. Patterns. Assumptions. Contradictions. Opportunities. Noticing what feels unresolved. Noticing where something does not quite fit. The smartest person in the room is no longer the one with the most answers, but the one with the sharpest insight into what is worth exploring.
Princeton historian D. Graham Burnett describes a moment that illustrates this shift. After a student spent time working with a generative AI system, they said, “I do not think anyone has ever paid such pure attention to me and my thinking and my questions.” That comment struck him. Because he realised something unusual was happening.
The student was not just learning new information. They were experiencing a new way of being with their own thoughts. A kind of attention that was patient, curious and non judgmental. Something many of us rarely experience, even with other humans.
This is where curiosity begins. In noticing what is arising within you as you explore. It is a way of meeting your thoughts before you rush toward answers. And in the age of AI, that kind of noticing has become a serious advantage.
Do You Want To Be Invited Back?
We’ve all sat next to someone at a dinner party who has no depth. The conversation remains superficial and the time seems to drag. But sit next to an interesting person and it’ll be midnight before you know it.
One of the simplest truths about AI is that it works almost the same way.
When the conversation has depth, context and genuine interest, the interaction becomes richer, more surprising and more useful. When it doesn’t, you quickly feel the limits. This is not because the AI is holding back. 
Every question carries a structure, a level of intent and a degree of self awareness. That is what shapes the quality of the exchange that follows.
If you want to be a repeat dinner guest, you need to bring more questions than answers. Do the same with your AI, and the interaction begins to feel less like a transaction and more like a collaboration.
To see how this plays out, imagine two people facing the same task.
Peter opens his AI and types, “Help me write a presentation.” The AI responds, quite reasonably, with a standard outline, some general tips, a neutral introduction and a list of suggested slides.
Helen opens her AI and types, “I am presenting to a sceptical board next week. Their main concerns are revenue protection and risk mitigation. My goal is to shift their perception from defensiveness to possibility. Can you help me design a presentation that speaks to those concerns while strengthening the core argument?”
The task is the same, but the outcome is not.
The AI can now detect the emotional context and the power dynamic. It understands the stakes. It can tailor the structure to the audience, propose a strategic framing, anticipate objections, surface blind spots and suggest a narrative flow that fits the situation.
Helen does not just get a presentation. She gets a partner in shaping the thinking behind the presentation.
What changed was not the intelligence of the system.
It was the depth of the question.
How Deeper Questions Grow From Greater Self Awareness
This is where the work you did in Chapter One becomes important.
Those ten curiosity questions were not just exercises in introspection. They were diagnostics. They revealed the deeper patterns behind the way your mind engages with the world.
Questions like “What am I avoiding asking because of what the answer might change?” or “What beliefs do I protect fastest when challenged?” or “What surprises do I explain away rather than investigate?” were not designed to make you feel uncomfortable. They were designed to make your patterns visible.
Your answers became a mirror. They showed you where your curiosity widens and where it collapses. They revealed the comfortable grooves your thinking naturally slips into. They hinted at the kinds of questions you ask quickly, and the kinds you avoid.
When you understand these patterns, your questions change. They become more precise, more honest and more connected to what you are genuinely trying to achieve. That is exactly what separates Peter from Helen.
Peter is asking for help from the surface. Helen is asking from a place of awareness. She knows something about her audience, about her goal and about the emotional landscape she is walking into. She is willing to articulate that. This level of clarity does not appear by accident. It grows from an increased awareness of how you think before you ask.
That is why deeper questions produce deeper collaboration. Better thinking produces better outcomes. Curiosity is how you free yourself from your most automatic patterns of thought. It is how you create space for ideas that do not yet exist. It is how you begin to grow beyond what you believe you already understand.
Practicality Tip: The Question Upgrade
To bring this into your own life, try a simple practice the next time you use AI.

You will feel the difference almost immediately. The response will feel less like a generic answer and more like a conversation that is tuned to your world. Over time, this small upgrade trains your mind to ask better questions everywhere, not only with AI.
Because curiosity does not begin with the machine.

Chapter 3: Working With the AI That Took Your Job

The Big Idea
Most of us grew up in a world where machines did not care who we were. They did not respond to our personality, our emotions or the clarity of our thinking. A machine was something you operated. It performed a task and you stayed the same.
AI breaks that pattern.
A dishwasher washes dishes. A lawn mower cuts grass. A refrigerator keeps things cold. Each does exactly what it was designed to do, no more and no less.
AI behaves differently. The quality of the interaction changes depending on how you engage with it. This creates a shift no previous generation has had to navigate. A shift from operating a tool to thinking alongside one.
You and I stand between two worlds. The world of machines that demanded nothing of our inner life and a new world where your inner dialogue shapes what the machine can do.
This chapter is about helping you step into that new world, especially when the first encounter with AI is painful. When it shows up not as a helpful assistant, but as the thing that helped take your job.
You are part of the first generation asked to collaborate with a thinking machine. The question is not whether you are ready. The question is whether you are curious enough to see what becomes possible when you do.
We will explore three people whose working lives were disrupted by intelligent systems. Their jobs changed or disappeared. Their stability fractured. Their future became uncertain in ways they never asked for.
What transformed their stories was not the disruption itself, but what they did next. Instead of turning away from the technology that disrupted their work, they turned toward it. And through that collaboration they discovered something they had never seen in themselves.
A Short Story About My Father
My dad lives in an aged care facility. He is ninety five at the time of writing. Mum passed away about ten years ago. A couple of years later, not wanting to be on his own any longer, he made the pragmatic decision to move somewhere with a community.
He has always been that kind of man. Practical. Observant. Definitive.
Even now he is one of the most active ninety five year olds you are likely to meet. Only a year ago he stepped down from a federal government advisory committee on aged care. He is more across news, technology and life in general than many people decades younger.
While I was working on this chapter, I met Dad and one of our sons, Toby, for lunch at his local club. Three generations around one table. Dad, born in 1930. Me in 1958. Toby in 1996. An age span containing almost a century of change.
Eventually, the conversation drifted toward AI and the pace of change. I said how much I was enjoying writing this book, and how collaborating with an AI had sharpened my thinking and improved the clarity of my work.
Dad listened carefully, then said something simple. “I think we already understand enough about ourselves. Why go digging. Why change what works.”
What caught my attention was not the resistance. It was the word “why.”
For my father, “why” has always been attached to survival and practicality. As a child he rode a horse called Gundy around the local area taking illegal bets to the bookie, the horse wearing a felt hat with two holes cut for its ears. During the war he dug a bomb shelter in the backyard. In the sixties he learned to fly a helicopter. Today he is the unofficial IT support for the other residents, helping them with phones and tablets.
His life has already required a great deal of adaptation. But his machines never asked him to understand himself. They asked him to act.
For you and me, the word “why” now carries a different weight. Used well, it can open life changing opportunities, because we are dealing with a machine that meets us at the level of our thinking.
Why My Father’s View Made Sense In His World
For most of the twentieth century, machines behaved in predictable ways. They repeated the task you taught them. They rewarded physical skill and procedural accuracy. They did not care about your mood or your inner life.
Toasters toasted. Engines turned. Typewriters typed. Saws sawed. I remember my dad teaching me how to use a saw for the first time and saying, “Let the saw do the work.”
A machine’s usefulness did not depend on what you thought about it. It depended on whether you followed the right steps. You could live a long, rich life without ever examining how your thinking worked. Machines did not require that of you, and for many people, life did not either.
So when my father asks, “Why do we need to understand ourselves more?” he is not rejecting growth. He is speaking from a lifetime where tools had no interest in who he was. They helped you do the job, and when the job was done you went home and forgot about them until the next day.
For good or bad, that world has changed.

Why AI Changes Everything
AI is the first machine designed to work with your inner voice, that constant stream of thoughts and questions in your mind. Not your hands. Not your manual techniques. Your inner dialogue.
It responds to the curiosity you bring, the clarity of your intention, the honesty of your self reflection and the flexibility of your perspective. It is shaped by your assumptions, your emotions and the questions you ask.
A hammer works the same no matter who swings it. AI does not.
It adapts to the quality of your reasoning. It takes the shape of your framing. It can surface assumptions you did not realise were there. It is guided entirely by the context you provide.
In that sense it is the first tool that mirrors the mind that uses it.
This is why my father’s world and ours can feel so different. He is far too sharp to dismiss AI as a gimmick. He recognises that it is powerful technology. But based on the life he has led, he reasonably questions whether this new machine will really make life better.
I think it can. Like any transformative technology, it carries serious risks as well as enormous potential. The risks are real, but they are not the focus of this book.
Our focus is on what AI reveals about you when you begin to work with it.
What This Means For You And Me
In the old model, machines delivered predictable output if you operated them correctly. Skill was learning the right technique and repeating it.
In today’s model, with AI as a thinking partner, the opportunity sits somewhere else entirely. You still need to know the basics of how to use the tool, but the real leverage comes from how you think with it.
If you treat AI like any other machine, it will behave like one. You give it a command, it gives you a result, and the interaction ends there.
But AI is not limited to that role. When you bring curiosity, context and a willingness to explore, the interaction changes. It stops behaving like a device and starts behaving like a thinking partner.
You do not need to become a philosopher or a technologist or fall in love with introspection.
You only need to recognise one simple truth.
AI responds to the way you think with it. Use it mechanically and it stays mechanical. Engage with it thoughtfully and it begins to stretch your thinking, surface new possibilities and work alongside you rather than simply responding.
If my father used AI, he would probably receive responses that match his worldview, and he might conclude that this is all the machine can do. He would not be wrong about his experience. But you and I have a different opportunity.
If you learn to think more reflectively, ask more deeply and look at yourself more honestly, the machine expands with you. It becomes a tool not just for doing more, but for becoming more.
When The World Shifts Beneath You
While my father’s world was shaped by machines that asked nothing of him, millions of people today are encountering machines that are quietly taking the tasks they once relied upon. Jobs are shifting fast. Roles that felt permanent are being replaced or reshaped. The disruption is real, and the fear is valid.
Someone who has just lost their job is not thinking about strategy. They are thinking about rent, food, family and identity. Many do not see their own strengths clearly. They have never needed to translate what they do into “skills.” Some have never been invited to see their value at all.
For these people, AI can become something unexpected. It can become the first voice that reflects their strengths back to them with clarity and respect. It can reveal capacities that have always been there, just never named. It can show pathways forward that no one in their life has ever pointed to.
This is where the potential of a thinking machine becomes real.
Story One: Sarah
Sarah worked in a hospital laundry for twelve years. She was steady, reliable and respected by her team. Her days were spent sorting linens, tracking machine cycles, deciding which loads needed to be fast tracked, training new staff and preventing small problems from becoming big ones.
One morning she was told the hospital would be moving to automated sorting systems. Her role would end in four weeks.
It felt as if the floor had fallen away. She told herself she had no real skills, that all she knew how to do was “laundry work.” She could not imagine another employer wanting what she could offer.
On the advice of a friend, she opened an AI chat and typed, “I just lost my job. I have only worked in the laundry. I do not think I have any skills. I am scared. What do I do.”
The AI did not contradict her feelings. It did not say “you are wrong.” It asked her to describe a typical day.
As she listed her tasks, the AI translated them into capabilities.
It pointed out that she was managing workflow, doing quality control, prioritising under time pressure, mentoring new staff and spotting problems before they escalated. It showed her how those strengths mapped to roles outside the hospital.
It suggested a handful of positions that matched her experience and local opportunities. It built a simple learning plan and helped her practise answering common interview questions. Together they wrote her first CV, in language that reflected the reality of her experience, not the narrow label she had given herself.
Sarah eventually applied for a logistics coordinator role. When she got it, she realised something important.
She had not become a different person. She had simply learned to see who she already was.
Story Two: Mark
Mark worked on the floor of a large distribution centre. His days were spent operating forklifts, helping with picking and packing, meeting hourly performance targets, reporting faults, keeping aisles safe and training new casuals.
It was not glamorous work, but it was essential, and he took pride in doing it well.
Then the warehouse installed automated picking robots. In a single announcement, his role disappeared.
Mark was not someone who would normally sit down to “explore his strengths.” He assumed he did not have many. In his own mind, he was the guy who moved boxes.
Still, one evening he sat at his laptop and wrote, “I lost my job today. I do not know what I can do next. I finished school early and I am not good at studying. Can you help me?”
Again, the AI started with the same question.
“Tell me what you actually did each day.”
Reluctantly, he described operating machinery, maintaining safe zones, reading load sheets, solving problems under time pressure, learning new systems, training new hires and coordinating with other shifts.
The AI reflected those tasks back as skills. Safety management. Operational awareness. Systems literacy. Team communication. Real time problem solving. Confidence with machinery. Fast learning adaptability.
Then it showed him three roles that suited those strengths. One of them was in transport operations support, a job he had seen around him but assumed he could never qualify for.
Over the next few weeks, he worked with the AI to rewrite his CV, practise interviews and understand the language of the new role. Two months later he applied. He got the job.
The robots had changed his employment, but they had not removed his value.
Story Three: Priya
Priya worked in the back office of a major bank. She was not customer facing. Few people outside her team knew her name. Every day she processed loan documents, checked compliance fields and monitored data accuracy.
She was good at it. Thorough. Reliable. The person others trusted with the details that kept everything running.
Then new software began rolling into her department. Automated document checks. AI based compliance scanning. Systemised error detection. She watched the screens change and tried not to worry.
One morning, her manager called a meeting. The bank was restructuring. Several roles would be dissolved. Hers was one of them.
She spent the next few days in shock. She had done everything right. She had built a reputation for accuracy. She had not imagined starting over in her forties.
On her sister’s suggestion, she opened an AI chat and typed, “I have always worked in back office processing. I lost my job today and I do not know what I am good at anymore. Is there anything someone like me can do next.”
The AI did not start with a list of careers. It started with clarity.
“Tell me what you actually did each day, and what decisions you made.”
She described verifying information across multiple systems, spotting inconsistencies, escalating risk indicators, communicating with internal teams and tightening processes.
Then came the turning point.
The AI reflected her work back to her in different language. It named skills in quality assurance, compliance interpretation, systems optimisation and structured communication. It pointed out that she was strong at recognising patterns and removing ambiguity.
Then it added a sentence that stopped her.
“These are entrepreneurial skills. They are also consulting skills. Would you like to explore either path.”
She had never thought of herself as entrepreneurial. But as she continued the conversation, she realised she had spent years quietly fixing broken systems around her. The AI suggested a simple service helping small businesses create order out of chaos in their workflows.
Together they sketched a basic offer. The AI helped her choose a business name, outline a simple website, describe a one page value proposition, define a handful of services and sketch a pricing model. It drafted scripts for introductory calls and helped her build a six week launch plan.
The AI did not build the business for her. It showed her that she could.
Six months later she had two paying clients and a sense of momentum she had never felt in her corporate role. She had not suddenly become more intelligent. She had learned to see the value of what she already knew.
Everything so far has been about seeing more clearly. What comes next is about what happens when you act from that clarity.


Practicality Tip: How To Talk To AI When You Have Lost Your Job
If you or someone you know has just lost a job, it is hard to think clearly, let alone think creatively. This is where AI can act as a calm, patient partner, but only if you know how to begin the conversation.
A simple way to start is to treat AI as a translator of your experience.
Begin by telling it, in plain language, “I have just lost my job. Here is what I used to do each day.” Then describe your day from start to finish. Do not worry about sounding impressive. List the real tasks. Opening up the shop. Checking machines. Dealing with customers. Filling forms. Fixing problems. Training others. Meeting deadlines.
Ask it to show you the skills hidden inside those tasks.
This alone can be powerful. You will see words you may never have used about yourself. Words like coordination, quality control, stakeholder communication or systems thinking. You will begin to see the shape of your strengths.
From there, ask, “What kinds of roles or jobs use these skills, especially ones that are realistic for me in the next six to twelve months.” Let it suggest a few directions. Notice what interests you and what does not.
If you are looking for another job, ask it to design a small learning plan that fits your life. Ask it to help you practise talking about your strengths in everyday language. Ask it to help you write a CV that reflects what you can really do, not just the title you used to have.
If you are even a little curious about working for yourself, ask how the same skills could become a simple service for others. Ask what kind of person would benefit from the way you naturally solve problems. Explore what a small, low risk version of that might look like.
You do not have to follow every suggestion. You do not have to decide everything in one day.
The point is not to let AI tell you who to be. The point is to let it show you what has been there all along.
In a world where machines are changing work faster than most of us feel ready for, the quiet challenge is this.
Do you let the disruption define you, or do you use the very machine that helped cause it to discover what else you can become. Here’s a couple of question templates that might help.
RE-SKILLING FOR A NEW JOB
Ask your AI:
1. “Here is what I did each day at my job. What skills are hidden inside these tasks that I may not see.”
 This surfaces strengths you did not know you had.
2. “What jobs or roles match the skills you just identified.”
 This opens new territory quickly.
3. “What three roles require only a small amount of additional learning for me to qualify.”
 This makes reinvention achievable, not overwhelming.
4. “Create a simple learning plan that fits my situation, time and confidence level.”
 This turns fear into structure.
5. “Help me practise explaining my strengths in clear language.”
 This builds confidence and identity.
6. “Write a CV and cover letter that reflect the real value of my experience and the role I want next.”
 This closes the gap between who you were and who you are becoming.
These six questions form a simple, powerful starting point for anyone navigating uncertainty. They do not require knowledge. They do not require confidence. They only require a willingness to ask.
That is how reinvention begins in the age of thinking machines.
Not by fighting the future, but by learning to collaborate with it.
RE-SKILLING TO START YOUR OWN BUSINESS
Ask you AI:
1. “Here is what I did each day at my job. What skills are hidden inside these tasks that I may not see.”
This surfaces strengths you did not know you had.
2. “What parts of my old job required me to solve problems without waiting for instructions?”
 Entrepreneurial capacity often hides inside moments of quiet initiative.
3. “When have people come to me for help because I am good at something they struggle with?”
 Most businesses begin with the ability to solve a problem someone else finds difficult.
4. “What tasks have I always done better, faster or more accurately than others, even if I never thought much about it?”
 Skill advantage is the foundation of value creation.
5. “What small improvements did I make at work that no one asked me to make?”
 Entrepreneurial thinking is often nothing more than a natural desire to make things work better.
6. “What problems have I seen in workplaces that frustrated me because I knew there was a better way?”
 Frustration is one of the clearest signals of entrepreneurial instinct.
7. “If someone paid me to help them with just one thing I am good at, what would that thing be?”
 This converts ability into service, which is the essence of a small business.
8. “If I could build a simple service that made someone’s day easier, what would it look like?”
 Simple is powerful. Many viable businesses start here.
9. “What strengths do I have that I never used fully in my old job?”
This reveals untapped capability, not reflected in job titles.
10. “What did I train others to do that they found hard at first?”
 Teaching is often a sign of niche expertise.
11. “If AI could help me build something of my own, what part of my experience would I want it to build on?”
 This invites imagination without pressure.





Chapter 4: The New Growth Mindset

The Big Idea
You cannot and must not rely on AI to be right all the time. This is one of the most important truths about working with any intelligent system. 
Not because AI is inherently unreliable, but because its reliability is conditional. It depends on your question, your context, your interpretation and the nature of the task.
When the conditions are clear and stable, AI can be precise. When you guide it well, it can be deeply insightful. When you give it a rich problem, it can be impressively analytical, breaking down patterns and possibilities in ways that feel almost effortless.
Yet it can also be completely wrong while sounding entirely confident. It can struggle in situations that depend on lived experience. It can miss nuance or intent that would be obvious to anyone in the room.
This is why the question of accuracy must be acknowledged, not feared.
The most helpful way to think about this is to treat your AI as you would any other colleague. Has every person you’ve ever worked with been right one hundred percent of the time? Of course not. So why hold a thinking machine to a standard no human has ever met.
For anything mission critical, you must check the premise, verify the details and validate the advice. Not because the AI is untrustworthy, but because accuracy is a shared responsibility between you and the system. Here is the part most people miss.
Those who expect AI to behave like a flawless oracle almost always end up with the weakest results. Those who approach it as a collaborator consistently get the strongest ones. The real risk is not that the AI makes mistakes. The real risk is assuming it has not.
That is why reflective questioning matters. That is why iteration matters. That is why every answer you receive should be treated as a draft, not a verdict until you decide it is.
How Hubris Shows Up With AI
Humans have a long history with hubris. AI does not. Yet the moment people begin using AI, that very human tendency shows up in predictable ways.
Some people assume they already know how to use the system and stop exploring too soon. Others dismiss suggestions the moment they feel unfamiliar. Many of us take the first interpretation as the correct one simply because it arrived quickly. Plenty of people use AI only to validate what they already believe, rather than explore what they do not yet understand.
We often treat uncertainty as a flaw rather than a doorway. We resist, sometimes fiercely, the discomfort of becoming a beginner again.
These reactions are profoundly human. They are protective. Most of the time they operate below the surface of conscious awareness.
When you encounter something unfamiliar, your brain tries to preserve its sense of competence. It leans on familiar patterns because they feel safe, even when those patterns quietly limit your ability to grow.
Psychology has a name for this; ‘cognitive dissonance.’ It is the tension you feel when your values, beliefs and behaviour are out of alignment. The inner dialogue sounds something like, “I know this is not good for me, but I am doing it anyway.” The discomfort is real, so the mind looks for ways to reduce it, often by retreating into familiarity rather than stepping toward change.

Psychology has a name for this: cognitive dissonance. It is the tension you feel when your values, beliefs and behaviour fall out of alignment. The inner dialogue sounds something like, “I know this is not good for me, but I am doing it anyway.” The discomfort is real, so the mind looks for ways to reduce it, often by retreating into familiarity rather than stepping toward change.
More than a century ago, psychologist Edward Thorndike described why this happens. He observed that the brain strengthens patterns that feel familiar, practiced or rewarding, and weakens those that feel effortful or uncomfortable. What we repeat becomes easier. What we avoid quietly fades.
This is why growth feels hard at first. New ways of thinking have not yet been exercised. Old ones have.
Like a good counsellor or psychologist, AI can help make these patterns visible, if you let it. It gives you a space to notice when you are defaulting to the same assumptions, asking the same kinds of questions, or reaching for the same conclusions, simply because they feel familiar.
With each more thoughtful question you ask, you begin practising a different mental move. Over time, those new patterns strengthen. Curiosity becomes easier. Reflection becomes more natural. The discomfort of not knowing loses its grip.
Growth, in other words, is not about forcing change. It is about practising a new way of thinking until it no longer feels new.
There are a few reasons why in AI this effect is so strong.
First, AI has no emotional stake in the conversation. It does not get offended. It does not need to protect its identity. It does not defend a worldview. Because it has no ego to protect, it responds directly to the logic and tone you use. That creates a very clean reflection of your patterns. You see your certainty, your hesitation, your assumptions and your fear of being wrong because the system does not hide them the way a human might.
Second, AI fills gaps by inference. If you imply something, it runs with the implication. If you skip a step, it skips it too. If you assume a fact without checking it, the system may accept the assumption and build on it. This reveals where your thinking jumps too quickly or leans on hidden beliefs. Not because the AI is trying to expose you, but because it follows the structure of your reasoning with remarkable fidelity.
Finally, AI responds almost instantly. That speed means you receive a reflection of your thinking before you have time to disguise it. You see in real time how your framing shapes the result. There is no lag, no polite hesitation, no social filter. That immediacy brings your patterns into the open.
AI acts like a mirror not because it intends to, but because of the way it works.
And this is where the opportunity lies.
The more aware you become of this dynamic, the more you can use AI to see your thinking clearly and adjust it. You begin to collaborate with it not just for answers, but for insight.
Cultivating Self Awareness In The Age Of AI
Your level of self awareness now plays a central role in how useful AI can be for you.
You need to be open enough, and honest enough, to recognise when your AI is reflecting your assumptions back at you. To achieve this it is important to understand that self awareness is not a fixed trait. It is a practice.
It grows in the small moments where you choose to pause before reacting. When your AI reflects your assumptions back to you, what matters is not the reflection itself, but your ability to see it as a reflection.
The first step is noticing your internal signals.
That rush to get on with things and accept the first answer. The subtle relief that comes from moving on quickly instead of asking a harder question. The tendency to jump to acceptance rather than explore whether your view, or the AI’s, might be incomplete.
These reactions are quiet. They rise up before you have words for them. This is why self awareness at the level of inner dialogue matters so much.
The second step is curiosity. Rather than assuming your interpretation is correct, ask yourself a simple question.
“What is this response revealing about how I am thinking?”
That single shift redirects your attention from the AI’s answer to your own mental patterns. It creates a small pocket of space in which insight can appear.
The third step is to test your assumptions deliberately. If the AI gives you an answer that feels too neat, too fast or too familiar, ask it for the opposite. Ask it to show you other angles. Ask what you might be missing. This is where the mirror becomes powerful. You begin to see where your first instinct may have narrowed the view.
One practice I use often when working with my own AI collaborator is to treat every substantial answer as material for a second round of thinking. I will take the initial response and ask the system to evaluate it as if it had been written by someone else. I ask for a summary of its quality, accuracy and usefulness. I ask it to name the strengths and weaknesses. If I agree with that assessment, I then ask for a new version of the answer, rewritten to keep the strengths, reduce the weaknesses and deepen the parts that matter most.
In other words, I use the AI to critique its own work, then iterate with it as a partner in improvement.
Finally, awareness grows when you are willing to tolerate small doses of discomfort. Not dramatic pain, just the mild unease that comes from admitting you may have jumped to a conclusion or missed something important. That moment is the real learning space. Most people move away from it. Curious people step into it.
Over time, this combination of noticing, questioning and testing makes your thinking more flexible. You begin to catch your biases as they arise. You see your assumptions more clearly. And the collaboration with your AI becomes less about chasing answers and more about understanding yourself.
This is what it means to cultivate awareness in the age of AI. It is not about being perfect. It is about being willing to see more of what is already there.
The Classic Mindset Model, Revisited
To understand how a growth mindset shows up in the age of AI, it helps to revisit the original idea.
Picture two students, Maya and Alex, sitting their first high school maths test. Both reach a difficult question they have never seen before.
Alex freezes. His inner voice closes around certainty. “I cannot do this. I am not good at maths.” He leaves the problem blank and moves on. The mistake feels like evidence of who he believes he is.
Maya feels the same discomfort, but she frames it differently. “I do not get this yet.” She circles the question, hands her paper in and later asks her teacher to walk her through the steps. A week later, after a little practice, she can solve that style of problem on her own.
It is the same moment. The same challenge. What changes is the meaning they place on difficulty.
This is the heart of Carol Dweck’s research.
A fixed mindset treats failure as a verdict.
A growth mindset treats it as information.
The difference is not the problem they face, but the story they tell themselves in the moment they face it.
Now Add AI
Now imagine the same two students, but with AI in their pocket.
Alex no longer leaves the question blank. He takes a photo, sends it to an AI tutor and gets the answer instantly. The page looks complete, but something important has not changed. The understanding did not move into him. It stayed outside, in the system.
Maya takes the same photo and asks, “Explain this step by step as if I am twelve.” The AI breaks it down. She then asks, “Give me a simpler version I can practise to learn the pattern.”
Within an hour she understands the concept, recognises the structure and can apply it in new examples.
They have access to the same technology. Only one of them grows.
That is the key idea. In the age of AI, mindset is not optional. It is the difference between outsourcing your thinking and expanding it.
The Neuroscience Of How We Respond To Challenges
One of the most striking findings in mindset research is that people with a growth mindset process mistakes differently at the level of the brain.
In several studies, researchers monitored brain activity while people made errors on simple tasks. People with a fixed mindset showed a brief spike of activity when they realised a mistake, followed quickly by disengagement. The brain registered the error, then moved on.
People with a growth mindset showed stronger, longer lasting activity in regions involved in attention, error detection and adaptive learning. Their brains stayed with the mistake just long enough to extract value from it.
This is not just motivational language. It is physiology.
Your brain responds to difficulty according to the meaning you place on it. When AI enters the picture, that meaning becomes even more important. Technology can accelerate the work, but it cannot accelerate your self understanding. You still need to bring curiosity to the exact moment where discomfort appears.
Why Mindset Matters More With AI
Once AI becomes part of your work, your learning and your thinking, you run headlong into a simple truth.
A fixed mindset sees AI as competition. The inner voice says, “It knows more than me. I cannot keep up.” A growth mindset sees AI as collaboration. It thinks, “I can learn alongside this. I can extend what I am capable of.”
The old version of the growth mindset focused on repetition and practice. The new version, in a world with AI, depends on reflection, questioning and the willingness to think side by side with an intelligence that is not your own.
It is no longer only about getting better at a task. It is about growing through the interaction itself.
This is why the most useful questions you can ask yourself change shape. Instead of wondering why someone else seems smarter, quicker or more creative, you begin to ask, “What can I learn from how they think.” Instead of assuming the problem lies in your ability, you ask, “What assumption am I making that limits me.” Rather than accepting your first interpretation, you notice what your AI reflects back and ask, “What pattern is it showing me.”
There is one important caution.
Working with AI can blur the boundary between feedback and validation. Because the system responds instantly, it can create the illusion that every answer is true, that every explanation is complete, that every suggestion is correct and that growth is something that can be automated for you.
This is where a healthy mindset matters most.
AI becomes powerful when you use it to explore ideas, question your assumptions and deepen your understanding. It is far less useful when you use it to confirm your worth or to avoid the discomfort of not knowing. Your real advantage sits in the reflection, not in the output.
When you bring curiosity and humility into the collaboration, AI becomes a remarkable partner for insight. When you come seeking shortcuts, it quietly reinforces the limits you are trying to escape.
By now, you can see that a growth mindset in the age of AI is not driven by optimism or perseverance alone. It is driven by awareness. It grows from noticing the subtle moments where your assumptions shape the interaction, and the even subtler moments where you have the chance to respond differently.
Real growth begins in those micro decisions. In the pause before your inner voice reacts. In the question you ask instead of the conclusion you grab. In the willingness to look at your own patterns with a little more honesty than you might offer yourself.
Awareness on its own is not enough though. You also need a simple way to apply it. A move you can use with any question, in any situation, no matter how complex or ordinary. Something that anchors the mindset in your daily interactions so the collaboration strengthens your thinking rather than narrowing it. That is where the next technique comes in.



Practicality Tip #4 The Power Of “Yet”
One of the simplest ways to build a healthy growth mindset, whether you are working with AI or navigating everyday life, is to harness a single small word.
“Yet.”
Whenever you encounter something you cannot explain, understand or solve, your instinct may be to conclude that you are not capable. “I cannot do this.” “I do not understand this.” “I am just not good at this.”
Those conclusions close the mind. They shut down curiosity. In AI terms they shut down the conversation before it has really begun.
Now add one word.
“I cannot do this, yet.”
“I do not understand this, yet.”
“I am not good at this, yet.”
That small addition keeps the learning space open. It implies that ability is in motion. It reminds you that what you are seeing is a moment in time, not a fixed identity.
You can use this directly in your collaboration with AI. When you hit something that feels beyond you, say it plainly.
“I do not understand this yet. Help me see it more clearly.”
“I cannot solve this yet. Break it into smaller steps.”
“I am not good at this yet. Show me an easier version to practise.”
The word “yet” keeps you on the path rather than stepping off it. It signals to your own nervous system that there is room to move, that growth is possible, that struggle is not the end of the story.
If you have never experimented with this before, try it in your next difficult moment, whether you are talking to your AI, your partner, your boss or yourself.
You might be surprised at how quickly one small word can change the direction of your thinking.
















Chapter 5: The Question Behind the Question

The big idea
The greatest misconception about intelligence is that it begins with answers. It does not. Intelligence begins with questions, because questions decide what the mind pays attention to and what it ignores.
I left school at 16 years of age. Not as most did, two years later. And by the time my contemporaries had finished university I had been working for six years. In fact, as they graduated I was working for the highest rating news and current affairs program in Australia. That’s why I can say with certainty that intelligence doesn’t begin with answers. 
Every decision you make, every idea you consider and every problem you try to solve begins with a question that frames the way you see the situation. Most people never examine those questions. They treat a question as nothing more than a doorway to an answer, rather than a structure that shapes what the answer can ever be.
Once AI enters the picture, the consequences of your questions become visible in a new way.
AI does not give you a generic response. It gives you a response that reflects the shape of your question, the assumptions in your framing, the emotional tone you bring and the direction your attention is already leaning toward.
This is why two people can ask the same AI about the same problem and walk away with completely different outcomes. Not because the AI changed, but because the question did.
To see this more clearly, imagine two people asking for help with the same challenge at work.
Brian asks, “Why does this always go wrong for me.”
The AI answers inside that emotional frame. The response is narrow, slightly anxious and focused on what is broken rather than what is possible.
Billy asks, “What am I not seeing here that would help this turn out differently.”
The AI meets that question with options, perspective and exploration. Nothing changed except the question, yet the entire trajectory of thinking shifted.
This is how AI reveals the structure of your mind. It does not simply reply to your words. It replies to the meaning behind them.
The power of questions was one of the discoveries that emerged unexpectedly while I was writing this book. I began assuming that AI was simply producing answers. Then I realised it was also reflecting the shape of my thinking back to me.
That realization changed the way I interacted with it.
Instead of taking responses at face value, I began studying them as clues that might lead somewhere deeper. Often it was just a phrase, a turn of language, a small piece of logic. But it was enough to hint that there was a more interesting question hiding underneath the one I had asked.
When I started treating answers this way, something shifted. Each answer stopped being the end of the exchange and became the beginning of a better question. Curiosity expanded, not because the questions became more complex, but because they became more honest.
I realised that questions are not just requests for information. They are tools for seeing your own mind more clearly.
If you want AI to help you think better, you need to learn to do two things at once. You need to hear the truth behind your question and then start asking questions about the answers that question produces.
This has been a constant theme in my own collaboration with AI.
When I was exploring the ideas of growth mindset and hubris, my first question was simple.
“Are these two barriers valid when working with AI, accuracy and hubris.”
The answer was useful, but not enough. So I began to question the answer itself.
“How do we explain this psychologically.”
“What is the scientific basis for this.”
“How can we help people recognise these reactions in themselves?”
The conversation moved from surface level agreement to deeper understanding. The follow up questions forced the AI to expand, justify and refine its first response. That was when the real thinking began.
The same thing happened when we developed the Opposite Question Test earlier in the book. My first move was essentially, “I don’t fully understand this. Can you elaborate your theory.”
Once the AI replied, I did not stop there. I asked, “What scientific basis is this claim built on?” Then, “How would we explain this without being overly technical.” And finally, “How can we show this with practical examples?”
That sequence is the essence of collaborative thinking. The first question opens the door. The second and third questions decide how far you walk through it.
Very often, the first question you ask is not the real question. It is the protective question, the familiar one, the surface level version of something more important that sits just beneath it. When you begin listening for that deeper question, your entire interaction with AI changes.
You stop trying to get answers that confirm what you already believe. You start looking for questions that reveal what you have not yet considered.
That is the beginning of transformation.
One of the simplest ways to uncover the deeper question is to examine the opposite of what you asked. If you say, “Why does this always fail,” then pause and ask, “What if this is actually succeeding in ways I have not noticed.”
Suddenly the assumption appears. You see that you had quietly decided failure was the only lens worth looking through.
The mind recognises contrast faster than analysis. When you consider the opposite frame, your assumptions become visible, and the deeper question emerges. When you bring that deeper question to your AI, the quality of the answer expands immediately.
This chapter is not just about asking better questions. It is about recognising that questions themselves are a kind of thinking technology. When your questions change, your intelligence changes. In the age of AI, this shift becomes even more powerful, because the system responds moment by moment to the shape of your inquiry.
Two people can stand in the same place and see entirely different landscapes. That is what happens with AI. Some treat it as an answer machine and receive shallow answers. Others treat it as a thinking partner and receive insight. The difference is not in the machine. The difference is in how that little voice inside your head is framing what you are doing and why.
When I went back and read the first question I asked while researching this book, it was simple.
“In the age of AI, when we all have access to the same information, how do you separate yourself from everybody else.”
That question set the direction of everything that followed. It eventually led to the pond story, and then to the realisation that curiosity, not information, is what separates one person’s experience from another.
When you ask a question from certainty, you usually get confirmation. When you ask out of curiosity, you get expansion. When you ask from a position of defensiveness, you get limitations. When you ask from openness, the collaboration becomes richer and more surprising.
This is why the quality of your questions is inseparable from the quality of your thinking. And why the quality of your thinking, over time, shapes the quality of your life.
As you begin to explore these ideas for yourself, notice the tiny moment before you type your question. Notice whether you are trying to protect an assumption, push away discomfort or seek validation. Notice whether you are asking from a desire to understand or a desire to conclude.
That moment is the hinge your intelligence turns on.
AI cannot give you self awareness, but it can accelerate it. When you learn to ask the kind of questions that illuminate the unseen corners of your mind, the collaboration between you and this fast thinking machine becomes one of the most powerful tools for growth you will ever encounter.
Practicality Tip #5 The Second Question Rule
One of the simplest ways to change the quality of your thinking is to change the question you ask after the answer.
The first question opens the conversation. The second question decides whether you stay on the surface or go deeper.
The next time you ask your AI something, do not move on after the first response. Pause and ask a second question about the answer itself. For example:
“What is the deeper question I should be asking here?”
or
“What assumption does my first question contain?”
You can also ask, “Show me an alternative way to frame this question that would lead to a richer answer.”
This small move shifts you from reaction to reflection. It turns a single answer into a doorway and trains your mind to look for the question behind the question.
Over time, that habit will matter more than any single piece of information you receive. Because real growth, especially in the age of AI, rarely begins with the first question.
It begins with the second.











Chapter 6: The Curious Leader

The big idea
Leadership is changing faster than most people realise. It used to be defined by knowledge, answers and authority. But in the age of AI, leadership is no longer about what you know. It is about how you think, how you listen and how willing you are to question your own assumptions.
Imagine a meeting room you have sat in many times before. It holds about fifty people. There is a window letting in a soft wash of natural light and a screen at one end waiting for slides and status reports. On most days it feels familiar and predictable. But on this day it feels tense. A little warm. The kind of meeting where everyone takes a sip of water they do not need simply because it gives their hands something to do.
It was in a room like this in 2014 that Microsoft’s senior leaders gathered as Satya Nadella stepped into the role of CEO. The company was not failing, but it was drifting. It was losing relevance, losing momentum and losing its sense of who it was. Divisions competed instead of collaborating. Bright people kept their ideas quiet. Meetings had become recitals of certainty rather than conversations about truth.
Most leaders in moments like this do the predictable thing. They talk about targets. They push for performance. They demand solutions.
Nadella did something different.
He looked around the table at the faces that had built one of the most influential technology companies in history and asked a single question.
“Are we a know it all organisation or a learn it all one.”
He asked it because he recognised something fundamental. The people in that room had become experts at knowing. Experts at defending. Experts at certainty. But not experts at learning.
The real problem was not strategy. It was a mindset. A culture shaped around answers rather than curiosity.
The question did not fix Microsoft overnight. But it became the hinge the company turned on.
And that moment matters today because it reveals a shift every leader must now face. What Nadella really asked was not about Microsoft. It was about the human mind.
Are you showing up to uncertainty as someone who must know, or as someone willing to learn.
In the past, leadership strength came from knowledge. The leader held the answers. The leader provided direction. The leader carried certainty so others could feel safe.
But AI breaks that model.
When a system can produce an answer in seconds, the value of a leader is no longer grounded in what they know. It is grounded in their ability to ask the questions that bring truth to the surface. Questions that widen thinking, reveal blind spots and create psychological safety. Questions that allow people to speak openly instead of hiding what they fear might expose them.
AI removes the illusion that knowing is the same as leading. The new frontier is not authority. It is awareness.
Leaders who cling to being the smartest person in the room will soon find themselves outrun by their own tools. Leaders who cultivate curiosity, humility and reflective thinking will find themselves amplified by those tools.
The technology is too fast. The pace is too intense. The uncertainty is too constant.
The only leadership advantage left is the one Nadella named, quietly and courageously, in a room brimming with tension.
Will you be a ‘know it all’ or a ‘learn it all’.
That question is not about Microsoft. It is about all of us.
Nadella’s question shifted Microsoft because it moved people from defending what they knew to exploring what they did not. But learning is only one dimension of curiosity-led leadership.
There is another.
It is curiosity that makes people feel safe enough to offer risky ideas. The curiosity that allows disagreement without damage. The curiosity that makes a team walk out of a room feeling stronger, not smaller, even when the conversation was hard.
Pixar learned this truth earlier than most.
Before Pixar became the creative force behind Toy Story, Finding Nemo and Inside Out, it faced a leadership challenge that is almost universal. People were afraid to look foolish. Brilliant artists and storytellers held back their best ideas because the culture rewarded polish over exploration. Meetings became performances. Mistakes were hidden. Risks avoided. Questions softened.
Ed Catmull, Pixar’s cofounder and president, recognised the danger long before AI existed. He knew that certainty suffocates imagination. He saw how ego silences innovation. And he realised that leadership was not about filtering ideas but about creating an environment where curiosity was safer than cleverness.
So he introduced a principle that changed everything.
“You are not your idea.”
Once people no longer tied their identity to the idea they presented, something opened. They asked braver questions. They challenged each other with less fear. They saw more, played more and imagined more. The films became better because the conversations became deeper.
Pixar’s transformation revealed an essential truth. Curiosity is not only a mindset. It is an atmosphere. It is the difference between a room where people protect themselves and a room where they explore together. A room where uncertainty is an invitation, not a threat.
Leaders cannot create psychological safety by declaring it. They create it by modelling it. At Pixar this meant showing vulnerability first. Asking questions without answers. Inviting critique rather than avoiding it. Letting ideas exist without attaching identity to them. Allowing people to say “I don’t know” without consequence.
Because curiosity collapses when ego swells. When people are afraid of looking incompetent, they stop asking questions. When they stop asking, they stop seeing. When they stop seeing, teams drift.
In the age of AI this risk multiplies.
AI can produce ideas instantly. It can generate unlimited alternatives. It can create the illusion of creativity without any of the discomfort true creativity requires. But ideas are not curiosity. Curiosity is the willingness to sit inside uncertainty long enough to find something real.
Pixar learned this early. Most teams will learn it the hard way.
But there is another realm where curiosity becomes even more essential. A place where the stakes are higher and the pressure heavier.
Curiosity matters most when the truth is uncomfortable.
Every leader reaches a moment where the easiest thing to do is to pretend everything is fine. Smooth the rough edges. Present optimistic slides. Avoid questions that expose what is fragile.
This is often the moment where organisations begin to fail. Not because the problem is unsolvable, but because the leader cannot bear to look at it.
In 2006, Ford executives gathered weekly for performance reviews. The charts were almost all green. Everything appeared on track.
Yet the company was collapsing. It was losing billions of dollars.
This is where curiosity becomes a scalpel. It cuts through illusion and reveals what certainty conceals.

So he asked a single question.
“If everything is green, why are we losing so much money?”
The next week, one executive arrived with a red chart. An honest one. A project was failing.
People braced for punishment. Instead Mulally applauded him. He did not ask, “Why are you failing.” He asked, “What do you need to fix it, and how can we help?”
That was the moment the shift began. The room moved from fear to openness. From performance to honesty. From concealment to curiosity.
Mulally understood something essential. People do not hide information because they want to deceive you. They hide information because they are afraid of what will happen when you see it.
Curiosity dissolves that fear. It replaces threat with inquiry. It creates a space where truth becomes visible instead of dangerous.
And this matters more now than ever.
AI can surface patterns, but it cannot surface courage. AI can flag anomalies, but it cannot ask the uncomfortable human question. AI can detect risk, but it cannot decide whether you are prepared to face it.
Leaders who cannot sit with the truth will misuse the tools that expose it. Mulally did not save Ford because he was the smartest person in the room. He saved Ford because he created a room where truth no longer had to hide.
This is the heart of curiosity.
Not seeking information.
Seeking honesty.
Across these stories, curiosity takes three forms.

Catmull showed that curiosity creates safety. It transforms groups of talented individuals into creative teams.
Mulally showed that curiosity is the beginning of honesty. It reveals what performance culture hides. It creates the conditions where truth can breathe.
These leaders did not succeed because they had the best ideas. They succeeded because they created environments where better ideas could emerge.
Curiosity is not a tactic. It is a way of seeing. A way of listening. A way of leading. It is the shift from “I already know” to “I am willing to see.”
In the age of AI this shift is not optional. AI does not ask whether you lead from awareness or habit. It simply reflects the thinking you bring to it.
Approach it with certainty and it reinforces your certainty. Approach it with fear and it amplifies avoidance. Approach it with curiosity and it expands your insight.
AI accelerates the leader you already are.
That is why Nadella’s question remains so important.
Are you showing up as a know it all or a learn it all.
Because in a world where intelligent systems can generate answers instantly, your leadership does not come from what you know. It comes from how you think, how you listen and how courageously you engage with uncertainty.
Curiosity is the last great leadership advantage. Not because it makes you smarter, but because it keeps you open. It is that openness that allows a leader to navigate change rather than resist it.

PRODUCTIVITY TIP 3 Use the Iteration Loop
Thinking with AI works best when you work in drafts.
The Iteration Loop helps you refine your thinking by improving one element at a time.
How to do it
Ask for a first version.
Ask the AI to analyse its own output.
Ask for an improved version based on that analysis.
Repeat until you reach clarity.
Example
Ask for three marketing strategies.
Ask the AI to identify the strengths and weaknesses of each.
Ask it to rewrite the strategies using only the strongest ideas.
The loop teaches you to think in drafts, not decisions.
That one shift improves clarity more than any shortcut.
PRODUCTIVITY TIP 4 The Power of Yet
The word “yet” keeps the learning window open.
When you tell yourself you cannot do something yet, you create psychological space for growth.
AI example
“I cannot understand this algorithm.”
“I cannot understand this algorithm yet.”
Life example
“I cannot have difficult conversations.”
“I cannot have difficult conversations yet.”
Yet it is the simplest way to reopen your thinking when it starts to narrow.
PRODUCTIVITY TIP 5 Use the Reframing Pause
Before you ask a question, pause for three seconds.
This short pause interrupts automatic thinking and gives your brain time to engage fully.
How to do it.
Take a slow breath.
Think about what assumption sits inside your question.
Ask yourself what the opposite question would be.
Then ask the question you truly mean.
Example
Before: “How do I get my team to stop resisting change”
After: “What fears might be driving the resistance, and how can I address the real concern”
A brief pause strengthens clarity.
A reframed question strengthens leadership.

GLOSSARY
Key terms as used in this book.
AI as Mirror
A way of describing how AI reflects the structure of your questions and assumptions back to you, revealing your patterns of thinking.
Assumptions
Beliefs embedded inside a question, often invisible until contrasted with an opposite frame.
Blind Spot
An unexamined belief that shapes a question or decision without conscious awareness.
Curiosity
A cognitive orientation that favours exploration over certainty. A trained skill, not a personality trait.
Curiosity Gap
The distance between what you know and what you want to know. This gap drives attention, motivation and deeper questioning.
Growth Mindset
A belief that intelligence and capability can be developed through effort, reflection and learning.
Inner Dialogue
The continuous internal narrative that shapes perception, interpretation and decision making.
Learn-It-All Culture
Leadership culture that prioritises learning, questioning and exploration over certainty and expertise.
Opposite Question Test
A reframing method that reveals assumptions by asking the inverse of your original question.
Psychological Safety
A shared team environment in which people feel safe to question, challenge and explore ideas without fear of judgement.
Reflective Questioning
A form of inquiry aimed at understanding your own thinking patterns rather than simply gathering information.
Thinking Partner
The role AI plays when used collaboratively to expand perspective and reveal cognitive patterns.
