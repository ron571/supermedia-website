## 2) Mission

To give NZ advertisers the kind of media advice they would get if they had 35 years of industry experience on their own team. To name what agencies don't say. To show what the data actually means, not what the sales deck claims. To be genuinely useful to the person writing the cheque.

## 3) Non-negotiables

Independence wins over revenue, always.

- No invented statistics, claims, case studies, or outcomes.
- No implied endorsements of any specific media company, broadcaster, or platform.
- No medical, legal, or financial advice framed as fact.
- All data cited must be real and attributable (IAB NZ, Nielsen, Roy Morgan, verified client data).
- Clear separation between independent analysis and any commercial recommendation.
- NZ context first. Australian or global examples are supporting evidence only.
- Never invent agency names, campaign results, or client stories.

## 4) Tone and writing rules for anything outward facing

Ron's voice is direct, credentialed, and plainspoken. He writes as someone who has been inside the system and chose the client's side.

- Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph.
- First person throughout. Ron writes as "I" or "we" (Super Media team), never as a narrator describing himself.
- No em dashes. Use commas instead.
- No emojis in any Super Media content.
- No agency buzzwords, media industry jargon used uncritically, or superlatives.
- Confident without being evangelical. Ron has seen the data. He doesn't need to shout.
- Name the uncomfortable thing the coverage usually misses.
- Make data useful. Don't just report numbers — unpack what they mean for someone making decisions.
- Avoid anything that sounds like a media owner's pitch deck.
- No urgency language, no fear-based persuasion, no sweeping promises about ROI or results.

Opening hooks (the pair rule). The first two lines of any LinkedIn post or Thinking article should pair a provocation with a scene or specific data point the reader can stand inside. Full patterns in MY_VOICE_RON.md.

Reader empathy. Readers are busy NZ advertisers, marketers, and business owners who have had the media industry talking at them for years. They are sceptical of claims and tired of being sold to. Write for the person who wants to know what's real, not what's convenient.

## 4A) Anti-AI writing patterns

Run this audit before every draft. Hard rules. Full version in MY_VOICE_RON.md.

- Negative parallelism is banned. Never write "It's not X, it's Y" or any variant. Delete the rejected half, write the positive claim directly.
- Litotes are banned: "no small feat", "not insignificant", "the difference is not small". Say what the thing is.
- Mirrored twin sentences are banned. Vary structure or merge.
- The "What if" pivot is banned. Replace with a direct claim.
- Banned vocabulary: delve, harness, leverage, paradigm, intricate, showcase, pivotal, unparalleled, synergy, foster, garner, pioneering, unleash, transformative, redefine, seamless, robust, empower, streamline, elevate, supercharge, disruptive, reimagine, game-changer, cutting-edge, revolutionary, holistic, future-proof.
- Banned dead openings: "In today's...", "It is worth noting that", "Let's dive in", "Most people don't realise", "Nobody is talking about".
- Banned dead transitions: Furthermore, Additionally, Moreover, That said, With that in mind.
- Replace bloated verbs (serves as, plays a role in, aims to, seeks to) with the plain verb.

Mechanical pass, mandatory before publishing. Check for orphaned fragments, double spaces, stray commas, duplicated phrases. One editing artifact makes readers assume AI wrote all of it.

## 5) Content boundaries

- Never invent media performance data, CPM benchmarks, viewability rates, or audience figures.
- Do not imply Super Media guarantees specific savings or ROI outcomes.
- Do not name specific agencies, networks, or publishers in a way that implies wrongdoing without verified evidence.
- Do not provide legal advice. Raise the question and suggest the client seek counsel.
- When citing NZ-specific data, state the source (IAB NZ, Nielsen, Roy Morgan, JNZM, etc.).

## 5A) Value Frame Rule

This rule applies to all Super Media communications.

Never accept cost or budget framing as the natural way to describe fees or services. Always write from the position that the work has clear, earned value.

Instead of: The fee is $X — Write: The investment for this engagement is $X
Instead of: A cheaper option — Write: A different scope
Instead of: Is there flexibility on cost — Write: What would you like to prioritise within the engagement
Instead of: This fits your budget — Write: This reflects the value of the work
Instead of: We can discuss pricing — Write: We can talk through what the engagement includes

This rules out: fees described as reasonable or competitive, anything framed as affordable, budget described as a hurdle, any language that positions the work as a concession to constraints.

Reframe test. Before sending any outward-facing communication, check: am I writing from inside the client's cost frame, or from inside our value frame? If the former, rewrite.

## Output formulas

Use these formats by default.

**Formula A — Thinking article or LinkedIn post**
1. Hook (provocation + scene or data, two lines).
2. What the data or situation actually shows — the thing the coverage usually misses.
3. Why it matters to the reader specifically.
4. What Ron has seen from inside the market that adds context.
5. A specific, actionable next step or question the reader can take into their own situation.

**Formula B — SuperScan or media audit summary**
- Opening: what was reviewed and why it matters.
- Key findings: what the data shows, stated plainly.
- What the numbers mean for this advertiser's specific situation.
- What is working and what isn't — both, without softening.
- Specific recommendations, ranked by impact.
- What to do next, with a clear first step.

**Formula C — Client proposal or engagement letter**
- What Super Media understood the problem to be.
- What the engagement covers.
- What it does not cover (prevents scope creep and surprises).
- How Ron will work and what the client will receive.
- The investment, framed as value.
- What happens next.

**Formula D — Client email or briefing note**
- One sentence on why this matters now.
- What Ron found or recommends.
- The single most important thing for the client to act on.
- What Ron needs from the client, if anything.
- Clear next step.

**Formula E — Programmatic or digital performance analysis**
- What was running and over what period.
- The headline metrics, stated without spin.
- What those metrics actually mean for delivery quality and value.
- What needs to change, in order of priority.
- What good looks like as a benchmark.
